State of Canadian Farming

A Snapshot of 2021

Currently the majority of Canadian farms can be found in south western Ontario as well as Alberta and Saskatchewan. The figure to the right (created by Mahsa), shows the absolute number of farms in each region according to the Agriculure Canada 2021 Census of Agriculure.

This shows the geographic distribution of farms accross the country, but does not give insight into the current demographics of farmers. The graph below (created by Graham) shows a breakdown aggregated to all of Canada of who is currently involved in farming.

The final data visualization for 2021 (made by Razz) shows the average age of Farm Operators in each Census Agriculture Region as of 2021. As can be seen in the image the lowest median age is 52 in Northern Quebec and the highest being 60 years old in Northern Saskatchewan. The overall average age for Canada in 2021 was 56.

Trends in Canadian Farming

Aging Farming Population

The chart to the right shows that in addition to the average age of Canadian farmers being quite high in 2021, the farm operator population is declining rapidly. There is a decline in both the overall number of farms, as well as the proportion of operators in younger age groups.

The graph shows that the majority of farmers in Canada are aging considerably, meaning that within the next few decades there will be far fewer people with the requistie skills to support Canada's growing food needs. This is particularily important as climate change is making areas in the global south less favourable to food growth, meaning that in order to keep our population fed Canada needs to be focusing on growing more food internally, not less.

No Succession Plans

In addition to the worrying trend of current food producers retiring, data from Agriculture Canada also shows that most farms currently operating today do not have a succession plan for their business. Which further points to a collapse, or at the minimum a decrease, in the ability for Canada to sustain the amount of food we are currently producing. As of 2021 of the 189,867 farm operators who filled out this section of the census, ~66% have no written or verbal succession plan in place for the business. This indicated that in addiiton to an aging population of farm operators, the majority of these operators do not have a successor available.

To recify this problem, the Canadian government needs to create incentives for young people to learn food production skills, as well as make the currently prohibitive cost of purchasing farm land more affordable.
